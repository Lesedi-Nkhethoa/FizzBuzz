# FizzBuzz

A Python implementation of the classic FizzBuzz problem — prints numbers from 1 to N, replacing multiples of 3 with "Fizz", multiples of 5 with "Buzz", and multiples of both with "FizzBuzz".

## History

FizzBuzz has its roots in a much older group word game, traditionally played by children to practice division and counting. Players take turns counting aloud, saying "Fizz" instead of any number divisible by 3, "Buzz" instead of any number divisible by 5, and "FizzBuzz" for numbers divisible by both.

The game found a second life in the tech industry in the mid-2000s, when it was popularized as a simple screening exercise for programming interviews. In 2007, software consultant Imran Ghory wrote a blog post suggesting FizzBuzz as a quick way to filter candidates who couldn't actually code, after noticing that many applicants struggled with even basic programming logic. Jeff Atwood (co-founder of Stack Overflow) helped popularize the idea further that same year on his blog Coding Horror, and it quickly became a staple "warm-up" question in technical interviews.

Despite its simplicity, FizzBuzz remains a popular exercise today for:
- Teaching beginners about loops, conditionals, and the modulo operator
- Quick coding interview warm-ups
- Demonstrating different coding styles and language idioms (there are countless creative/obscure solutions online)
